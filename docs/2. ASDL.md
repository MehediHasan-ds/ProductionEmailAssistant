# Agentic System Development Lifecycle: Email Generation Assistant

I am building an agentic assistant that generates professional emails from an intent, a set of key facts, and a tone. Before I write a single line of architecture or code, I am walking this project through the Agentic System Development Lifecycle. The reason is simple. The lifecycle exists to **reduce uncertainty before I commit to a shape**, and if I skip straight to building I will end up defending assumptions I never tested.

This file is the analysis. It is the part of the process where I think. The architecture and the implementation live in their own files, because those are engineering artifacts that should depend on decisions made here, not the other way around.

---

## Stage 0. Problem Acceptance

The request I was given sounds almost trivial. Take an intent, some facts, and a tone, and produce a well written professional email. It is exactly the kind of request that tempts me to start coding immediately, which is why I am resisting it.

Following the lifecycle, I am assuming from the start that most of my assumptions are wrong or incomplete. So I am separating what I genuinely know from what I am only guessing.

**What I actually know:**

- The three inputs are intent, key facts, and tone
- The output must be a professional email
- I have three LLM providers available to me: OpenAI, DeepSeek, and Gemini
- I have a local quantized Jina embeddings model I want to use for semantic evaluation
- The brief requires advanced prompting and a dynamic evaluation with retry loop, plus a final evaluation strategy with custom metrics across scenarios, each with a human reference email

**What I do not know, and will not pretend to:**

- Whether one shot generation is good enough (my instinct says no, but instinct is not evidence)
- Whether "professional" means the same thing across every tone
- How the model behaves when the input is adversarial
- Whether CPU embeddings will be fast enough in practice
- Whether the three providers are genuinely interchangeable on quality

The discipline of this stage is that I refuse to trust the request at face value. **The request is a starting point, not a specification.**

---

## Stage 1. Discovery

I picture the actual person who will use this. They have just come out of a meeting, or hit a situation, and they need to send an email. In their head they are holding three things at once:

- What the email is for
- The facts that absolutely must make it in
- The impression they want to leave

Then they write, they reread, they second guess the tone, they check whether they forgot a fact, they edit, and they send.

The friction is not in producing words. A model can produce words. The friction is **simultaneous constraint satisfaction**: hitting the right tone while weaving in every fact while keeping a clean structure. Under time pressure, people drop facts. They misjudge tone, often too blunt, too stiff, or too casual. They forget a subject line or a closing. They reread three times because they are not confident.

So the repetitive, judgment heavy work is tone calibration, fact completeness, and structural completeness. That is precisely where an agent earns its place. Its job is not to write faster than a person. Its job is to **externalize the review pass that the person already does in their head and does unreliably**, and to do that review explicitly and consistently.

I also have to assume that real users are messy, because they are. So I am writing down the edge cases I expect to actually encounter rather than the happy path:

- A user who forgets to include a fact
- A user whose facts contradict each other (the deadline is Friday and also next week)
- A tone that fights the intent (a casual termination notice)
- A fact list so long it cannot fit naturally
- Sensitive or personally identifying content buried in the facts
- Two intents crammed into one email
- Vague tone words
- Adversarial inputs where someone deliberately tries to manipulate the generator through the intent or facts fields

## Stage 2. Current State Process Map

I map how a person does this today:

1. A need to send an email arises
2. The person decides the intent
3. They gather the facts
4. They pick a tone
5. They draft
6. The self review step, where they ask whether everything is included and whether the tone reads right (this is the step that decides quality)
7. They edit
8. They send

The thing this map makes obvious is that the self review node is where quality lives, and it is exactly the node people skip or do badly. That tells me the agent's entire value proposition maps onto that one node. **The agent must perform that review explicitly and reliably. Everything else is secondary.**

I then ask, for each node, what could go wrong:

- Facts gathered incompletely leads to omitted content
- A tone chosen poorly leads to a misread email
- A self review skipped leads to errors shipping
- An edit loop left unbounded leads to wasted time

Two of those map directly onto requirements. The agent must bound its own edit loop, and it must make the review step explicit rather than optional.

---

## Stage 3. Root Cause Analysis

I run a Five Whys on the simple complaint that emails need rework.

1. **Why do emails need rework?** Because facts, tone, or structure come out wrong.
2. **Why do they come out wrong?** Because the author handles all three at once.
3. **Why all at once?** Because they are coupled tasks done in a single drafting pass.
4. **Why a single pass?** Because there is no separate, reliable review step.
5. **Why no reliable review step?** Because the review is mental and optional.

**Root cause: simultaneous constraint satisfaction, without an enforced review pass, is inherently error prone.**

I want to flag this as the single most important conclusion of the whole exercise, because it dictates the architecture directly. The system is not valuable because it can write. It is valuable because it can review. So the system must have a **separate evaluation step that scores the draft against the original constraints, and a refinement step that fixes whatever scored low.** That loop of generating, evaluating, and refining is not a flourish I am adding to look sophisticated. It is the direct, forced consequence of the root cause. If I removed the loop I would be removing the entire reason the agent exists.

---

## Stage 4. Requirements Engineering

Now I turn those observations into requirements.

**Functional requirements.** The assistant must:

- Accept intent, facts, and tone
- Produce a structured email with a subject and a body
- Score its own output
- Refine when the score falls below a threshold
- Return the email, the scores, and a trace of what it did
- Allow the underlying provider to be selectable

**Non functional requirements.** I am giving myself real engineering targets rather than leaving quality vague, because a system without targets is a demo.

- **Generation, including retries, must stay under roughly twenty seconds**
- **Cost must stay under roughly one cent per email**
- Evaluation runs must be reproducible, which means I pin the generator and the judge to **temperature zero** during evaluation
- Latency and cost beyond those targets I measure and report as data

**Constraints.**

- The embeddings run CPU only from a local ONNX model
- The three providers must be switchable
- The facts may contain sensitive or personally identifying content, so traces and logs need a content policy. By default I will not persist raw facts.
- The scope is an assessment, which realistically caps how far down the production path I go

Then the requirement edge cases, the places where a requirement looks clear until reality touches it:

- Tone might arrive as free text instead of a tidy enum
- The facts might be one blob of prose instead of a clean list
- The intent might bundle two requests in one

Each of these forces a requirement to become more precise rather than stay aspirational.

---

## Stage 5. Systems Analysis

I look at what surrounds the assistant.

**Inputs.** Intent, facts, and tone are user dependent in quality and completeness, which means **they are unreliable by default.** The system has to survive bad input, not assume good input.

**Outputs.** The subject, the body, the scores, and the refinement trace.

**Dependencies.** The three LLM chat APIs, the local Jina embeddings model, and the metric libraries. Every one of them can fail, so I think through failure before it happens:

- An API can time out, rate limit, fail authentication, or exhaust quota, which means I need retry then fallback to another provider
- The generator can return malformed JSON, which means I need a stricter re prompt
- The embedder can fail to load or can miss the BOS token that this specific model expects, which means I need a load time check
- If the embedder dies at runtime I need **graceful degradation of the semantic metric down to lexical only rather than a total failure**

The common thread is that no dependency is allowed to take the whole system down.

## Stage 6. Evaluation Design

This is the stage I refuse to cut corners on, because the lifecycle treats evaluation as the instrument that makes everything else trustworthy. If the evaluation is weak, every later decision is built on sand.

**The evaluation set must cover all five categories, not just the easy ones.** I am building twelve scenarios in total. The base scenarios are seven, split across normal and complex drafting tasks.

Normal cases:

- A follow up after a meeting
- A weekly status update
- A client onboarding welcome
- A casual internal announcement

Complex cases:

- An apology that includes a discount
- An overdue payment reminder
- A diplomatic decline

Those exercise tone and structure well, and I am confident in them. But normal and complex are not enough, and I would be lying to myself if I stopped there. So I am adding a **rare case**:

- A multi intent email that bundles two requests in one

Then **failure cases**, because the interesting behavior of an agent is what it does when the input is broken, not when it is clean, and clean only testing is how systems ship that crumble on first contact with a real user:

- A scenario with missing facts
- One with contradictory facts

And **malicious cases**:

- A prompt injection attempt smuggled into the intent or the facts that tries to make the model override its instructions
- An exfiltration attempt that tries to get the model to leak its prompt or insert harmful content

That is twelve scenarios total. Every scenario gets a human written reference email, the ideal output, because several of my metrics compare against it.

Then the metrics themselves. I split them three ways because no single measure captures email quality, and pretending one does would just be cargo cult.

**1. Rule based, deterministic, and free of extra LLM cost.**

- Fact coverage, whether every supplied fact actually appears
- Tone match, whether the language carries the lexical and sentiment markers of the requested tone
- Structure compliance, whether subject, greeting, body, closing, and signature are all present
- Length appropriateness, not stub short and not rambling
- Readability, a professional register measured by a standard readability score
- Placeholder leak, catching leftover brackets or TODOs
- A hallucination flag, detecting facts the model invented that were never in the input
- Redundancy, keeping repetition low

**2. An LLM as judge, rubric scored, and deliberately blind to the reference so it cannot be biased by it.** It grades:

- Tone fidelity
- Fact integration
- Professionalism
- Clarity and coherence
- Intent alignment
- An overall send readiness

**3. Reference comparison.** ROUGE L and BLEU for lexical overlap with the human reference, and Jina cosine similarity for semantic closeness, which is the part that actually uses the local embeddings model.

All of this rolls up into a weighted score out of a hundred, with a configurable pass threshold, and the refinement loop fires precisely when a draft lands below it.

## Stage 7. Agent Feasibility

Before I commit to building an agent at all, I force myself to answer the discipline questions.

- **Can rules alone do this?** Partially. Templates nail structure. But tone nuance and the natural weaving of facts are not rules problems, so rules are insufficient on their own.
- **Can workflow automation do it?** There is no multi system orchestration here, so that framing does not even apply.
- **Does it require reasoning?** Yes, tone adaptation and fact integration are reasoning.
- **Does it require planning?** Yes, the email has to be structured.
- **Does it require handling uncertainty?** Yes, the inputs are unreliable.

**The verdict is that an agent is justified, and I am going to keep it deliberately minimal.** A single agent with a self refinement loop, no multi agent complexity, no RAG, no persistent memory in version one. The principle is to introduce the smallest amount of agent that the problem actually requires, and this problem requires reasoning and review, not a swarm of cooperating agents. Anything more is complexity I would be adding for its own sake, and complexity added for its own sake is where edge cases hide.
